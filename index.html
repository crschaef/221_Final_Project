Independent Films Outcompete Studios in the Box Office Through Strategy, Not Quantity

By: Caroline Schaefer

'Titanic', 'The Dark Knight' 'Barbie,' 'Avatar' & 'Avengers: Infinity War'
20th Century Fox/Marvel/Disney/Warner Bros

Over the past twenty years, the box office has seen new trends in revenue amongst independent film distributors despite traditional domination from big studio releases.

Data shows that this new presence from smaller studios is a result of more effective marketing and release strategies, some of which have not only met larger distributors’ blockbuster fortunes, but triumphed over them as well.

A study from Box Office Mojo shows trends in the market between 2005 and 2025. The data displays a fluctuation of film releases each year. In earlier years, film output ultimately maintained overarching heights as major studios distributed large numbers of films per year.

This multi-release strategy is utilized as a way for studios to maintain not just one, but several spots in the box office. And in the early 2000s, it paid off. Major distributors developed heavy markets for feature films, making a chunk of their profit from opening weekend success.

However, smaller studios have shown improvement in creativity, advertisement, and audience engagement, allowing them to reach impressive numbers that surpass larger blockbuster returns. The difference is, as data shows, less aggressive marketing.

According to Box Office Mojo, Disney made an average of $235 million across 154 films, with 31% of their revenue coming from opening weekend promotions. But Newmarket Films, a smaller and less known distributor, almost doubled their revenue with only one release and lower opening weekend contribution.

This rise of independent films is merely a strategy. Instead of banking on marketing and advertisement, their money goes towards production quality, even if it means their films are released in silence.

Joseph Taglieri, Producer and Supervising Editor for Ghost Adventures, has firsthandedly experienced what independent studios have achieved in the last several years. Noticing the latest trends of film advertisement, he believes independent studios are outsmarting the industry.

“There is no advertising for independent films versus studio films. They don’t put any money into advertising,” Taglieri said. “But for feature films, it’s insane. If you have a $100 million film, they’re putting $100 million into advertising. That’s why it takes so much to make a profit when you shoot a film.”

Examining the relationship between revenue and film over these two decades, independent distributors have prevailed. Newmarket Films garnered the highest income across a singular release, similar to other indie studios. This illustrates how more intimate targeting and efficiency tactics have become more profitable than pumping out 100+ films in a short amount of time.

Having a big role in intimate production, Taglieri emphasizes how creativity is key during the editing process. Taglieri believes this is what gives smaller studios more of a connection and appeal to audiences.

“Box office expectations do come up in our meetings, but we really just chase the creative… We make the show creatively for what we believe is right, which in my opinion, is why the show does well,” Taglieri said. “We’re always just chasing what we think is a good story.”

According to recent data, this might just be major distributors’ new mission. In 2025, the average number of film releases dropped by almost half than numbers produced ten to fifteen years ago. Are large studios now focused on resembling the charm of indie films?

For Elisa Gambino, Film Producer and Director at One Production Place, this charm of independent production never came from chasing the numbers. Her 2023 documentary Every Day After follows the story of thirteen-year-old Jary and his struggles with suffering from a cleft. The film won several awards, and qualified for Academy Award consideration.

Gambino and her team traveled all over the world during production, where they harnessed creative styles devoted to giving the cleft community a voice. Storytelling is their main focus, representing the shift away from mass-audience targeting and towards fostering personal connections.

But filmmaking under smaller budgets can be risky. Along with other independent filmmakers, Gambino often deals with tight resources, no guarantee of distribution, and uncertainty of financial support. The film industry is unpredictable, as things can change drastically and quickly.

In 2020 and 2021, the COVID-19 outbreak caused film production to plummet and the industry to experience a sharp decrease in annual revenue. It was the lowest number of film releases in 16 years, as all filmmaking was paused to ensure safety.

But the industry rapidly regained motion in the years to follow, with newfound methods of grasping audiences’ attention. This is an example of how the market is adaptable to changing environments, something that small-scale distributors are harnessing in order to maintain significance in the box office.

While major studios continue to dominate with multiple releases and huge franchises, independent filmmaking is on the rise. As data shows, success in the box office is not solely based on a film’s total gross. Strategy, connection, and imagination have proven to be more valuable. For creators like Taglieri and Gambino, the ability to create a meaningful and authentic film defines its overall success.
